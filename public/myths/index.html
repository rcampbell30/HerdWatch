For Worried Parents

The Autism Myth

The MMR vaccine does not cause autism. This page explains how one fraudulent study created a dangerous myth — and what the science actually says.

The fear is understandable. Every parent wants to protect their child. When someone tells you a vaccine might cause harm, it is natural to worry.

But the MMR-autism link is not supported by the evidence. It began with a tiny, conflicted and later-retracted paper, then grew through media amplification and online misinformation.

How The Myth Began

February 1998
The Lancet paper
Andrew Wakefield and co-authors published a paper about just 12 children. It was not designed to prove that MMR caused autism.
2004
Conflicts exposed
Investigative reporting revealed serious conflicts of interest connected to legal action against vaccine manufacturers.
2010
Retraction and strike-off
The Lancet fully retracted the paper, and Wakefield was struck off the UK medical register after findings of serious professional misconduct.
Aftermath
A myth outlived the paper
Large studies involving hundreds of thousands of children have found no link between MMR vaccination and autism.
The Key Fact

The original paper was tiny, uncontrolled and later retracted. Bigger, better-designed studies do not support the MMR-autism claim.

What The Science Says

Multiple large studies across different countries have found no link between MMR and autism:

  • Danish Cohort Study, 2019

    657,461 children followed for years; MMR vaccination was not associated with increased autism risk.

  • Vaccine and autism meta-analysis, 2014

    A meta-analysis covering more than a million children found no relationship between vaccination and autism.

  • NHS MMR vaccine guidance

    Official UK guidance on the MMR vaccine, who should receive it and how to arrange vaccination.

Why The Myth Spread

The myth spread because it targeted a real parental fear at a confusing moment. Autism signs often become noticeable around the same age children receive MMR, so coincidence was made to look like cause.

Correlation is not causation

If two things happen around the same age, that does not mean one caused the other. Large studies are designed to test that question properly.

The Bottom Line

The claim that MMR causes autism did not begin with strong science. The larger evidence says the same thing repeatedly: MMR does not cause autism.
