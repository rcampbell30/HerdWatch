Named & Accountable

Andrew Wakefield

A British fraudster who fabricated a study linking MMR to autism, destroyed his medical career, and helped trigger a global collapse in vaccine confidence that has killed children. He has never apologised.

Andrew Jeremy Wakefield was born in England in 1956, the son of a neurologist and a GP. He trained as a surgeon and researcher, rising to work at the Royal Free Hospital in London. He had every advantage: a medical family, a first-rate education, institutional access and public trust.

He used it to commit one of the most consequential frauds in medical history. He fabricated data to link the MMR vaccine to autism, concealed financial conflicts that ran to hundreds of thousands of pounds, and subjected vulnerable children to invasive unnecessary procedures. His 1998 paper in The Lancet was not a mistake. It was deliberate.

Measles was nearly eliminated in the UK when Wakefield published. It is now endemic again. The UK lost its measles elimination status in January 2026. Children have died.

The Record

1996
The Secret Payment Begins
Before any study is published, Wakefield is retained by personal injury lawyer Richard Barr, who is building a case against vaccine manufacturers. Wakefield receives £435,643 plus expenses — paid through the Legal Aid Board. He never discloses this to his institution, his co-authors, or The Lancet. He also applies for a patent on a rival single measles vaccine — one that would benefit commercially if the combined MMR were discredited.
28 February 1998
The Paper is Published
Wakefield and 12 co-authors publish in The Lancet: a case series of just 12 children, claiming a new syndrome linking MMR vaccination to bowel disease and autism. At a press conference the same day, Wakefield — going beyond what the paper itself claimed — calls for the suspension of the triple MMR vaccine. He recommends single vaccines instead. He does not mention he holds a patent on one. Within days, the story dominates British news. MMR vaccination rates begin to fall.
1998–2003
The Coverage Collapse
UK MMR uptake falls from 92% to as low as 73% nationally, and below 50% in parts of London. A generation of children grows up unvaccinated. This is not a gentle decline — it is a collapse driven directly by sustained media amplification of Wakefield's claims. Measles cases, which had numbered in the tens annually, begin climbing.
2004
Brian Deer Exposes the Fraud
Investigative journalist Brian Deer publishes his Sunday Times investigation revealing Wakefield's secret payments from lawyers. Ten of the paper's 13 co-authors issue a partial retraction. The Lancet editor acknowledges that had the financial conflicts been known, the paper would never have been published. Wakefield denies everything. Deer continues investigating for years, eventually uncovering that the clinical data in the paper had been systematically falsified — children's medical histories altered to fit the desired narrative.
2006
First Measles Death Since 1992
A child dies from measles in the UK — the first measles death in 14 years. The coverage gap created by Wakefield's fraud has built a large susceptible population. 449 measles cases are confirmed in the first five months of the year alone.
January 2010
Struck Off — Found Guilty of Dishonesty
The UK General Medical Council rules that Wakefield acted "dishonestly and irresponsibly" and with "callous disregard" for the children in his study. The children were subjected to invasive procedures — lumbar punctures, colonoscopies, MRI scans — without clinical justification, and without proper ethical approval. Wakefield is struck off the medical register. He can no longer practise medicine in the United Kingdom.
February 2010
The Lancet Fully Retracts the Paper
The Lancet issues a complete retraction, stating the paper's findings are "utterly false." Editor Richard Horton says the journal was "deceived." The British Medical Journal later publishes a detailed series concluding the study was not merely flawed but an elaborate fraud. The data had been manipulated child by child: onset dates changed, diagnoses altered, symptoms invented.
2012–2013
Swansea: 1,219 Cases
A measles outbreak in Swansea infects 1,219 people — overwhelmingly children and teenagers who were not vaccinated during the years of Wakefield-driven panic. One person dies. Public Health Wales calls it a direct consequence of the earlier coverage collapse. The age profile of those infected maps almost exactly onto the unvaccinated cohort created by Wakefield's paper.
2016
Vaxxed — The Propaganda Film
Having relocated to Texas, Wakefield directs and promotes Vaxxed: From Cover-Up to Catastrophe — a film alleging a CDC conspiracy to suppress evidence linking MMR to autism. The film is pulled from the Tribeca Film Festival after protests from the scientific community. It nevertheless reaches millions online and becomes a touchstone for the global anti-vaccine movement. Wakefield is now not a disgraced researcher in obscurity. He is a celebrity.
2023–2024
Birmingham & London: 2,911 Cases
England records its worst measles year since 2012: 2,911 confirmed cases, driven by outbreaks in Birmingham and London. The areas hardest hit are those with the lowest MMR coverage — communities where vaccine hesitancy, often rooted in misinformation traceable to Wakefield, has persisted for decades. One death is confirmed in 2024.
January 2026
UK Loses Measles Elimination Status
The World Health Organization confirms that the United Kingdom has lost its measles elimination status. A country that had brought measles to the edge of eradication has retreated. Coverage nationally stands at around 85% — well below the 95% required for herd immunity. The arc from Wakefield's 1998 press conference to this moment is direct.

The Children in the Study

The 12 children at the centre of Wakefield's study were not anonymous data points. They were real children, referred to the Royal Free Hospital by parents who believed the MMR had harmed them. Brian Deer's investigation, drawing on hospital records obtained through the GMC proceedings, found that the paper misrepresented every one of them.

Child 2 was described in the paper as having "behavioural symptoms" appearing one week after vaccination. His medical records show symptoms were noted months earlier. Child 11 was listed as having "regressive autism." His records contain no such diagnosis. Child 12 had no autism diagnosis at all. In case after case, dates were changed, symptoms were invented or omitted, and the clinical picture was bent to fit the conclusion Wakefield had already promised his legal clients.

Beyond the data fabrication, the children underwent procedures — colonoscopies, lumbar punctures, MRI scans under general anaesthetic — that were not clinically indicated. The GMC found that Wakefield subjected the children to these procedures without ethical approval, driven by his research agenda rather than their medical need.

What the GMC Found

Wakefield acted with "callous disregard for the distress and pain" of the children. He took blood samples from children at his son's birthday party, paying them £5 each. He later joked about this publicly.

The Cost

This estimate reflects measles deaths occurring in populations where coverage fell as a direct result of the MMR-autism myth Wakefield created and amplified. The case fatality rate for measles is 0.1–0.2% in high-income countries and up to 5% in low-income settings. The UK alone contributed to tens of deaths. Globally — through the US, Europe, and the wider anti-vaccine movement Wakefield seeded — the true number is likely higher. We use 10,000 as a floor, not a ceiling.

Where He Is Now

Wakefield did not retreat into obscurity. He relocated to Texas, where he reinvented himself as a martyr of the medical establishment. He charges speaking fees. He sells books. He directed a film. He cultivated relationships with political figures sympathetic to anti-vaccine positions.

He has never acknowledged the fraud. He has never apologised to the families of children who died from measles. He has never retracted his personal recommendations — despite every one of his scientific claims being systematically demolished, and despite the paper that made them being formally declared fraudulent by its own publisher.

He sued Brian Deer for defamation in Texas. The case was dismissed. He was ordered to pay all parties' costs.

Still Active in 2025–26

Wakefield remains a prominent figure in anti-vaccine circles internationally, appearing at events and on social media. His influence extends to the United States, where measles killed two children in 2025 — the first pediatric measles deaths there in more than a decade.

The man who started the myth is 68 years old, living comfortably, and still spreading it.

The Journalist Who Exposed Him

"This was not a rogue scientist who made honest mistakes. This was a man who set out to commit fraud and succeeded — for years — while children suffered the consequences."
— Summarising the findings of Brian Deer's decade-long investigation for the Sunday Times and BMJ

Brian Deer is the investigative journalist whose work, published in the Sunday Times and later the British Medical Journal, is the primary reason Wakefield was exposed. His reporting began in 2004 and continued for nearly a decade — facing legal threats from Wakefield, obstruction, and intense pressure.

Deer obtained the medical records of the 12 children through the GMC proceedings and compared them line by line with what the paper claimed. What he found was not inconsistency or poor methodology. It was systematic falsification: dates changed, diagnoses altered or invented, clinical histories rewritten to fit a predetermined conclusion.

His work is publicly available. The evidence is there for anyone to read.

Further Reading

Brian Deer's full investigation is published at briandeer.com and in the British Medical Journal (January 2011 series). It remains the most comprehensive account of how the fraud was constructed and how it was exposed.

History will record Andrew Wakefield clearly. A man from a medical family, given every advantage, who used a position of public trust to commit fraud for money, and whose lie has reverberated for nearly three decades in the deaths of children who had no voice in any of it.

HerdWatch names him because he should be named. Every time someone Googles him and finds the evidence instead of the myth, that is accountability of a kind.
