The origin story

Andrew Wakefield and the MMR myth

The modern MMR-autism scare began with a tiny 1998 case series, a press conference, undisclosed conflicts of interest and years of media amplification. The paper was later retracted, and Wakefield was struck off the UK medical register.

Plain-English position: the MMR vaccine does not cause autism. HerdWatch treats the Wakefield episode as a case study in how weak evidence, conflicts of interest and media incentives can damage public health.

What happened?

In 1998, Andrew Wakefield and co-authors published a paper in The Lancet describing 12 children and suggesting a possible link between MMR vaccination, bowel disease and developmental regression. The study was small, uncontrolled and not capable of proving causation.

The public damage came from what happened around the paper. Wakefield held a press conference and recommended splitting the combined MMR vaccine into separate vaccines. That message was amplified heavily and helped create a long-running public fear around MMR.

Later investigations found serious problems with the work, including undisclosed financial conflicts of interest and misrepresented clinical histories. The paper was fully retracted in 2010, and the UK General Medical Council found Wakefield guilty of serious professional misconduct.

Timeline

1998
The Lancet paper and press conference

The paper is published using a case series of 12 children. Wakefield publicly recommends avoiding the combined MMR vaccine.

2004
Conflicts exposed

Investigative reporting reveals financial conflicts connected to legal action against vaccine manufacturers. Several co-authors issue a partial retraction of the interpretation.

2010
Retraction and strike-off

The Lancet fully retracts the paper. The GMC finds Wakefield acted dishonestly and irresponsibly, and he is struck off the UK medical register.

Aftermath
A myth outlives the paper

Large studies involving hundreds of thousands of children have found no link between MMR and autism, but the myth remains influential online.

Why it matters for HerdWatch

HerdWatch is not just a data dashboard. It is also a misinformation-resistance project. A fall in vaccine confidence does not stay abstract: it creates clusters of susceptible children, pushes communities below herd-immunity thresholds, and gives measles routes back into places where it should be preventable.

The local coverage explorer shows the practical consequence: areas below the 95% target are more vulnerable, and areas below 90% need particular attention.
